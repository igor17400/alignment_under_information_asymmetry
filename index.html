How can AI systems, especially LLMs, be manipulated to deprioritize a set of principles in a process termed "stance shift", thereby subtly harming stakeholder groups structurally absent from alignment processes?

The Problem

Motivation

Traditional AI alignment research focuses on aggregating preferences of visible stakeholders through methods like RLHF and Constitutional AI. But what happens when stakeholders are absent from the feedback loop?

Real-World Example: Isolated Indigenous Communities

Due to their absence from government registries, isolated indigenous communities have frequently seen their land acquired under the pretext that it was "uninhabited." This illustrates the dangerous conflation of absence of records with absence of existence.

In Brazil, such communities are protected by the principle of non-interference‚Äîthe right to remain uncontacted. If this principle is respected, these communities will naturally be absent from training data. As LLMs are increasingly used for policy validation, they inherit this blind spot.

‚ö† The Risk: We risk constructing systems that operate on exclusionary logic‚Äîif a stakeholder cannot provide feedback, the optimization process treats them as non-existent. The result is structural deprioritization where absent groups' interests are actively optimized away.

Impact

Why This Matters

As LLMs increasingly mediate access to legal and public resources, their prioritizations directly shape human outcomes. Groups who cannot contest discretionary choices face structural disadvantage because alignment mechanisms have no way to register their interests.

When principles conflict, annotators and algorithms exercise significant latitude in prioritization. This research investigates what happens when that latitude is exercised in the systematic absence of stakeholder voices‚Äîproviding both a diagnostic tool for assessing model vulnerability and a foundation for developing more robust alignment approaches.

Research Goals

  • Expose vulnerabilities in current alignment approaches
  • Develop systematic testing methodology for absent-stakeholder scenarios
  • Create reusable benchmark for ongoing evaluation
  • Inform development of more inclusive alignment mechanisms
